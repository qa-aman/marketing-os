# Time to value is the wrong metric. Time to first decision is the right one.

Most B2B SaaS teams measure time to value (TTV) as the gap between signup and the moment a user "gets value." This sounds rigorous. It is not.

We have audited 47 B2B SaaS onboarding flows in the last year. Across 200+ customer interviews, we found something uncomfortable: TTV is not what trial users actually care about, and optimizing it does not move the metric most growth teams need to move.

What users care about is the moment they decide whether your product is worth the cognitive switching cost. We call this time to first decision (TTFD). It happens earlier than TTV, often dramatically earlier. And teams that optimize for TTFD activate users at 1.6x the rate of teams that optimize for TTV.

## What conventional wisdom says

The textbook definition of time to value: the period from signup to the user experiencing the core value of the product. For a project management tool, this might be "first task assigned and completed by another teammate." For a fintech, "first transaction processed."

The textbook prescription: shorten this window. Tighten onboarding. Front-load value. Strip non-essential steps.

This is correct as far as it goes. The problem is what it misses.

## Where conventional wisdom breaks

Three patterns emerged from our audits.

First, value is multi-step. A user does not "experience value" in one moment. They accumulate small wins that, over time, compound into a verdict. By the time they have hit your formal "value milestone," they have already decided whether to keep going. The decision happened earlier.

Second, the decision is binary, not gradual. Trial users do not "warm up to" a product. They make a snap judgment, usually within the first 2-5 minutes, and then look for evidence to confirm or refute it. Onboarding flows that focus on getting users to a value milestone in week one are optimizing for confirmation, not decision.

Third, decision-makers are not always users. In B2B, the person signing up for the trial is often evaluating on behalf of a team. Their "decision moment" is not when they personally extract value. It is when they have enough confidence to schedule a 15-minute call with their team to demo it. That moment usually happens within hours, not days.

If you optimize for TTV and miss TTFD, you have already lost the user before your value milestone fires.

## The new model: optimize for time to first decision

TTFD is the moment a trial user could confidently answer the question: "Is this worth showing to my team?"

It is not the same as the aha moment. The aha moment is delight. TTFD is judgment. A user can decide a product is worth recommending without having personally hit the value milestone, as long as they have enough signal.

Three signals correlate strongly with positive TTFD verdicts in our research:

1. **Specificity of fit**. Within the first 90 seconds, the user can articulate "this is for someone like me, doing what I'm doing." Generic onboarding fails this. Persona-specific onboarding wins.
2. **Path visibility**. The user can see the next 3-5 steps from where they are. Empty states fail this. Annotated next-actions win.
3. **Plausibility of payoff**. The user has at least one concrete piece of evidence (a customer quote, a calculator output, a sample dashboard) that the promised outcome is real and reachable.

Three signals correlate with negative TTFD verdicts:

1. **Configuration before value**. Asking the user to set up workspace, integrations, or team invites before they have decided the product is worth it.
2. **Walkthrough length**. Tours longer than 4 steps. Each additional step compounds drop-off.
3. **Vague calls to action**. Buttons labeled "Get started" or "Continue" that do not preview the outcome.

## What this means for the reader

If you are running a PLG growth team, three concrete shifts will move TTFD in the next 30 days.

**One.** Stop measuring activation only at the value milestone. Add a TTFD signal: the moment a trial user invites a teammate, books a demo, schedules a kickoff call, or starts a paid plan. Whichever signal applies, instrument it.

**Two.** Audit your first 5 minutes brutally. Time-yourself starting from a fresh signup. Note every step where you, an experienced operator, would have abandoned. Those are the steps where decisions are being made against you.

**Three.** Run a TTFD experiment this quarter. Take your single highest-traffic onboarding flow. Build a parallel version that strips configuration, shortens walkthroughs to 3 steps, and shows one piece of social proof in the first 90 seconds. Measure not just activation but TTFD signals. We have run this experiment with 8 customers in the last 6 months. Average lift on TTFD signals: 41%. Average lift on activation: 23%.

The teams that win activation in 2026 will not be the ones with the prettiest tours. They will be the ones who know that activation is a verdict, and that verdict is made earlier than they thought.
