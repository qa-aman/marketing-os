# How Stytch built a $2M inbound pipeline in 8 months

## The customer

Stytch is a developer authentication platform serving engineering teams at growth-stage and enterprise companies. Their product is technical, their buyers are developers and engineering leads, and their previous go-to-market was entirely outbound and community-driven.

Caitlin Jameson, Head of Marketing at Stytch, joined in mid-2025 as the company's first dedicated marketing hire. She had a team of one, a mandate to build inbound pipeline, and 12 months before the next board review.

## The challenge

Stytch's marketing problem was not awareness. Developers knew Stytch. The problem was that awareness was not converting into pipeline. Traffic was coming in, mostly from the developer community, but very little of it was converting to qualified opportunities.

Three contributing factors:

**1. The ICP was too broad.** Stytch had positioned for any company that needed authentication. In practice, their best customers were engineering leads at Series B and C SaaS companies with 20-150 engineers who were ripping out a homegrown auth system to reduce maintenance burden. This buyer had a specific situation, specific urgency, and a specific alternative they were migrating from. The broad positioning addressed none of that.

**2. The content was developer-education, not buyer-education.** Stytch's existing content (docs, changelog, community posts) was excellent for current users. It did nothing for the VP of Engineering who was evaluating Stytch against Auth0 and hadn't started a trial yet.

**3. There was no nurture path.** Visitors who didn't start a trial had no reason to stay in touch. No newsletter, no email program, no content that gave them a reason to return.

> "I knew what needed to be built. I just didn't have the bandwidth to build it from scratch while also figuring out what the strategy should be." - Caitlin Jameson, Head of Marketing, Stytch

## The solution

Clearpath engaged in August 2025 on a 9-month Demand Gen Build.

**Month 1-2: ICP and messaging.**
We ran a win/loss analysis on the previous 24 closed-won deals and 12 lost deals. The pattern was clear: Stytch won when the buyer was mid-migration from a homegrown system and needed to ship auth in 4 weeks without building it themselves. We tightened the ICP to this situation and rewrote the homepage, the comparison pages, and the core email sequences around it.

**Month 2-5: Content program.**
We launched a biweekly newsletter for engineering leaders (not developers - the decision-maker, not the implementer). The editorial position: practical guides for engineering teams making infrastructure decisions at scale. Within three months it had 2,400 subscribers with a 47% open rate.

Simultaneously, we produced a content series targeting the specific migration situation: "Migrating from homegrown auth: what to decide before you start," "Auth0 vs Stytch: a decision guide for engineering leads," "The 6-week auth migration playbook." Each piece targeted a specific search intent and a specific buyer stage.

**Month 4-8: Distribution and conversion.**
LinkedIn organic for Caitlin (founder-voice content on engineering leadership decisions) and a structured lead nurture sequence for newsletter subscribers and content downloads. Trial signup sequence rewritten to compress time-to-first-API-call.

## The results

**Pipeline generated in 8 months: $2.1M in qualified opportunities.**

Key metrics at month 8:
- Inbound as % of new business: 38% (from 0%)
- Newsletter subscribers: 2,400 (open rate: 47%)
- Content-attributed demo requests/month: 22 (from 0)
- Time to first qualified opportunity: 41 days from kick-off
- Organic search sessions: +310% from baseline
- Average sales cycle for inbound leads: 24 days (vs 48 days for outbound)

Stytch extended the engagement for a second phase focused on paid amplification of the content that was already producing inbound.

## What made it work

Three things, in order of importance:

1. **Tightening the ICP first.** Every piece of content, every email, every ad became more specific once we knew exactly who we were writing for and what situation they were in. Specificity is what makes content convert.

2. **Writing for the decision-maker, not the user.** Stytch's developer community was already well-served. The gap was content for engineering leads who hadn't started a trial yet. Filling that gap opened a new audience.

3. **The newsletter as infrastructure.** The newsletter gave Stytch an owned channel to compound. Every new subscriber became a potential future customer. 8 months in, 22% of demo requests were coming from newsletter subscribers who had been reading for 3+ months before converting.
