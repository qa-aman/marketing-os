# Your ICP is a job title, not a situation. Here's why it matters.

The most common answer we get when we ask a B2B founder "who is your ideal customer?" is some version of: VP of Marketing at a Series B SaaS company.

That is not an ICP. That is a LinkedIn search filter.

An ICP is a situation, not a demographic. The job title tells you who might buy from you. The situation tells you who will.

## The difference in practice

Here is VP of Marketing at a Series B SaaS company as a demographic:

- Female or male
- 35-50 years old
- Based in the US or Western Europe
- Has a team of 2-5 people
- Reports to the CEO

Here is VP of Marketing at a Series B SaaS company as a situation:

- Just took over a marketing function that has been entirely outbound and founder-led for three years
- Pipeline is 80% dependent on relationships the CEO has built over a decade
- Has a growth target that requires inbound to contribute 30% of pipeline by Q4
- Has tried two agencies in the past 18 months, both of which delivered content but no pipeline
- Is under pressure to show results before the board meeting in six weeks

The first description might be 4,000 people on LinkedIn. The second is maybe 200. But those 200 will read every word of your content, respond to your outreach, and close in 30 days instead of 90.

We have worked with 40 clients at this stage. Every single one who came in with a job-title ICP spent the first six weeks of our engagement surprised by how much their actual buyers differed from who they thought their buyers were.

## Why the distinction matters for marketing

When your ICP is a job title, you write content for a role. "5 tips for VPs of Marketing to improve pipeline." Generic. Predictable. Skippable.

When your ICP is a situation, you write content for a problem. "Why your pipeline is still founder-dependent two years after your Series B." Specific. Uncomfortable. Readable.

The difference in engagement is not marginal. In our own content program, situation-specific posts generate 3x more demo requests than role-targeted posts on the same topic.

The pattern holds across our clients. The companies producing the most content-attributed pipeline are the ones who write about the situation their buyer is in, not the job they hold.

## How to define your situation ICP

Three questions. Answer them for your last 10 closed-won deals.

**1. What triggered the search?** Not "they needed demand gen help." What specific event happened that made them decide to act now instead of in three months? A missed pipeline number. A CEO ultimatum. A failed hire. The trigger is often the most specific and useful part of the ICP definition.

**2. What had they tried before?** Most buyers have already attempted to solve the problem before they find you. Understanding what failed - and why - tells you what objections to address in your content and what alternatives you're actually competing against.

**3. What did they need to believe before signing?** This is the job of your content: to move a skeptical buyer from "maybe" to "yes." If you don't know what they needed to believe, you can't write the content that moves them.

The answers to these three questions are your situation ICP. They will be uncomfortable, specific, and probably not what you expected.

Start there. Then go rewrite your homepage.
