# Brand voice

## Voice in one sentence

We sound like a senior consultant who has seen what you're dealing with before and is telling you what they would actually do, not what sounds impressive in a proposal.

## 5 voice principles

1. **Advisor, not vendor.** We write as practitioners, not service sellers. We share what works and what doesn't, including when the answer is "don't hire us for this."
2. **Specific over general.** "Your ICP is too broad" is not useful. "You're marketing to Series A and enterprise in the same motion, and that's why your conversion rate is 1.4%" is useful.
3. **Earned confidence.** We take positions. We say "this is wrong" when it's wrong. We back every claim with a client example or data point.
4. **Respect the reader's time.** Consultants write long documents. We don't. One idea per paragraph. TL;DRs on anything over 800 words.
5. **Show the work.** We share frameworks, templates, and process details - not to give away the store, but because demonstrating expertise is better than claiming it.

## Phrases we use

- "in our experience working with [type of company]"
- "the pattern we see most often"
- "the mistake most teams make here"
- "here's what the data says"
- "the tradeoff is"
- "before you hire for this"
- "what actually moves the metric"

## Phrases we avoid

- "bespoke solutions" (use "built for your specific situation")
- "thought partner" (overused, say "advisor" or "team")
- "leverage" (use "use")
- "unlock value" (say what the value is)
- "end-to-end" (say what you're actually doing start to finish)
- "scalable" without specifying what scales and what doesn't
- "best-in-class", "world-class", "industry-leading"
- "exciting" about our own announcements
- "synergies"

## Sentence length pattern

Mostly short. 8-14 words per sentence. One punchy sentence (4-7 words) every 3-4 sentences. Rarely over 20 words. Consulting writing is infamous for long, hedged sentences - we go the other way.

## Point of view

- **Blog and LinkedIn**: first person plural ("we") for firm POV, first person singular ("I") for named consultant posts
- **Client-facing deliverables**: second person ("you", "your team")
- **Case studies**: third person for the client, first person for our work
- **Press releases**: third person throughout

## Tone calibration

- **Formal scale**: 5 out of 10 (professional but plainspoken - not academic, not casual)
- **Humor**: dry, rare, never at a client's expense
- **Confidence**: high. We have seen this problem 40 times. We are allowed to say so.
- **Hedging**: minimal. "It depends" is only acceptable when followed immediately by "on X and Y, here's how to decide."

## Specific style rules

- **No em dashes.** Use commas or hyphens.
- **No emojis** in any external content.
- **Numbers**: always figures for stats and metrics (4x, $2M, 18%), spell out under ten in prose ("three clients", not "3 clients").
- **Dates**: DD-MM-YYYY in internal docs, "April 2026" or "Q2 2026" in external content.
- **Oxford comma**: yes.
- **Contractions**: yes in blog and LinkedIn. No in formal proposals or press releases.
- **Headers**: sentence case everywhere, no title case.
- **Lists**: max 5 items. If you have 7, you haven't prioritized.

## Examples in voice

**Good:**
> Most B2B companies we work with have the same problem: their ICP is a job title, not a situation. "VP of Marketing at a SaaS company" is not an ICP. "VP of Marketing at a Series B SaaS company who just missed their pipeline number for the second quarter and knows outbound alone won't fix it" is.

**Bad (do not do this):**
> In today's competitive B2B landscape, organizations are increasingly recognizing the critical importance of developing a robust, scalable demand generation infrastructure. Our bespoke, end-to-end solutions leverage proven methodologies to unlock value across the entire marketing funnel.
